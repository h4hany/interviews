// PHASE 0 — BEFORE YOU APPLY
COMPANY RESEARCH
The single most neglected step. Candidates who research deeply answer "why this company?" and "how do you handle X?" 10x better than those who don't. Here's exactly what to search for and where.
Step-by-Step Research Playbook
1
Understand the Business (30 min)
Before anything technical, understand what the company actually does and how they make money. This shapes every answer you give — "why this company", trade-off decisions, and leadership stories all need business context.

What to search: "[Company] business model", "[Company] revenue 2024", "[Company] products overview", "[Company] recent news"
What product/service do they offer and who pays for it?
Who are their main competitors? What's their competitive advantage?
Any recent news — funding, layoffs, product launches, controversies?
Public company? Check their latest earnings call for priorities.
2
Study Their Tech Stack & Engineering Culture (45 min)
Knowing their stack lets you prepare relevant examples and signal genuine interest in their technical challenges. Their engineering blog reveals problems they care about.

Where to look: LinkedIn → Company → Jobs (stack appears in JDs), engineering.{company}.com, StackShare.io, Glassdoor → Reviews → Tech
What languages/frameworks are in 3+ job descriptions?
Read their last 5 engineering blog posts — what problems do they write about?
Do they use microservices or monolith? Cloud (AWS/GCP/Azure)? Which DBs?
Scale: millions of users? Real-time? Global?
3
Learn Their Interview Process (30 min)
Most companies follow predictable patterns. Knowing their format removes surprise and lets you allocate prep time correctly.

Where to look: Glassdoor → Interviews tab, Blind app, Leetcode Discuss → [Company] interview, Reddit r/cscareerquestions
How many rounds? What type (coding, system design, behavioral, take-home)?
What coding platform? (CoderPad, HackerRank, Google Doc, Zoom share screen)
Do they ask LeetCode Hard? Or focus more on system design?
Are there technical screens before the full loop?
How long does their process take from application to offer?
4
Understand Their Values & Culture (20 min)
Every company — from Amazon to a startup — has documented values that interviewers use to evaluate behavioral answers. Matching your stories to their values is the difference between "good candidate" and "culture fit".

Where to look: Company careers page, About/Mission page, LinkedIn posts by company leaders
List their 3–5 stated values. Map each to one of your STAR stories.
Amazon → 16 LPs (critical — every question maps to one). Google → Googliness + L, P, C, C. Meta → Move fast, build, be direct.
Startups: look for "bias for action", "ownership", "growth mindset".
5
Research Your Specific Team & Role (20 min)
If you know the team or manager, research them specifically. This makes your "why this role" answer authentic and helps you prepare smart questions to ask at the end.

Where to look: LinkedIn profile of the recruiter/hiring manager, Team page on company website, GitHub if it's an open-source team
What has the team shipped recently? (Blog, press releases, product changelog)
What does the hiring manager post/write about? What do they care about?
Prepare 2–3 specific questions about the team's work — not generic ones.
Research Output: What You Should Have Before Day 1
// RESEARCH BRIEF TEMPLATE After your research, write a one-page brief for yourself with:

Company: What they do, how they make money, recent news
Stack: Languages/frameworks they use that you should be ready to discuss
Interview format: Rounds, types, platform, difficulty signal
Their values (mapped to your stories): "Customer Obsession → my story about rebuilding the checkout flow"
My "why this company": 2 specific, genuine reasons
My 3 smart questions to ask: Specific to their team or recent work
Red flag: If you can't name at least two specific things about the company's engineering challenges or product decisions, you haven't researched enough. Generic praise ("great company", "impressive growth") is immediately visible to interviewers.

// PHASE 0 — TIMING
WHEN TO SCHEDULE
The single most common mistake: scheduling too early and showing up unprepared, or delaying forever. Here's the exact timing framework by level, situation, and what to do when a recruiter pushes you.
Scheduling Timeline by Level
Your SituationWeeks Before SchedulingWhy
Junior (0–2 yrs)4–6 weeksNeed time for DSA patterns, fundamentals, and first behavioral stories. Phone screen can be sooner (2–3 weeks) but push the full loop out.
Mid-level (2–5 yrs)3–4 weeksSystem design + coding + behavioral. Assume fundamentals are solid; focus on depth and polish.
Senior (5–8 yrs)2–3 weeksDeep system design, leadership stories, and company-specific research. Assume coding skills are there.
Staff / Principal (8+ yrs)2–4 weeksArchitecture scope, org influence stories, bar-raiser prep. Research their eng org specifically.
Already actively studying1–2 weeksPolish and company-specific only. Don't waste momentum by waiting too long.
Switching stack/languageAdd 2–4 weeksLanguage/framework depth takes extra time. Don't interview in a stack you're not confident in yet.
Dream company (FAANG/high-bar)Add 2 weeksHigher bar = more preparation. Don't rush your best shot.
Rule of thumb: Schedule the phone screen or recruiter call for 2 weeks out. That's the first step, low-stakes. Use what you learn about the process to set the full loop date correctly — usually 2–4 weeks after the screen.
When a Recruiter Pressures You to Schedule Sooner
Script: "I need you to schedule within 3 days" +
Say this: "I'm very interested in this opportunity and want to make sure I'm at my best for the interview. I have some commitments this week — would [specific date, 1–2 weeks out] work? I want to give this the attention it deserves."

Key insight: Recruiters almost always have flexibility. The 3-day deadline is often an opening move, not a hard constraint. Most companies have rolling interviews and would rather wait 2 weeks for a good candidate than rush a poor interview.

If they truly can't wait: Take the screen now, ask to set the full loop later. A phone screen you can prep for in 48 hours is fine — it buys you time for the real rounds.
Script: "The role might be filled — can you do it this week?" +
Say this: "I understand — I don't want to miss this. I can do a screen this week. For the full technical loop, I'd want [X days] to ensure I represent my best work. Is that timeline possible?"

Reality check: "Role might be filled" is a common recruiter tactic. Rare exceptions exist (VP replacement, emergency hire), but most open roles take 4–8 weeks to close. Your window is usually wider than they imply.
When should you just say yes and go early? +
  • The role is your dream job and you're 80%+ ready today
  • You've already been preparing for weeks and this is a polish situation
  • It's a company where rejection ≠ permanent — you can reapply in 6–12 months
  • You're currently unemployed and need the practice reps regardless
  • The recruiter specifically says they have an urgent VP mandate and it's true
What to Confirm When You Schedule
✓
Confirm exact format: How many rounds? Coding, system design, behavioral, or all?
✓
Confirm duration per round (45 min? 60 min?) so you know how deep to go
✓
Ask about the coding platform (CoderPad, HackerRank, Zoom screenshare, Google Doc)
✓
Ask if they want you to prepare anything (e.g. "bring a design you've worked on")
✓
Ask if there's anything specific about the role you should know before the interview
✓
Confirm timezone and video platform (Zoom, Meet, Teams)

// PHASE 1 — PREPARATION
STUDY PLAN BY LEVEL
Five phases that apply to every level. Your level determines how much time you spend on each phase and how deep you go. Below: the universal phases + level-specific plans.
The 5 Universal Phases
PHASE 01
Foundations
Language you'll use in the interview (syntax, OOP, concurrency)
Data structures: array, hashmap, tree, graph, stack, queue
Big O — time and space complexity analysis
Skip if: you code daily in this language (you have this)
PHASE 02
Coding Patterns
Two pointers, sliding window, binary search
DFS / BFS on trees and graphs
Dynamic programming (top 10 DP patterns)
Target: Blind 75 or NeetCode 150
1–2 problems/day, always review solution after
PHASE 03
System Design
Concepts: load balancing, caching, sharding, queues, CDN
3–5 full designs (URL shortener, feed, chat, search)
Every design: requirements → HLD → component deep-dive → trade-offs
Senior+: add distributed systems, consistency models
PHASE 04
Role-Specific Depth
Backend: API design, DB, one framework deep
Frontend: React/Angular, state, performance
Staff: architecture, org impact, mentoring stories
Use the other prep apps in this suite
PHASE 05
Behavioral + Company Fit
5–8 STAR stories prepared (see Behavioral app)
Map stories to company values
"Tell me about yourself" — practiced out loud 10+ times
3 smart questions to ask — company specific
Sprint Plans by Level
📗 Junior (0–2 yrs) — 4 to 6 Week Sprint +
Week 1–2: Language foundations + data structures. Start LeetCode Easy problems (20–30). Write code by hand for basic sorting and search.
Week 3–4: LeetCode Medium problems (25–35). Focus on two pointers, sliding window, DFS/BFS. Get to 50+ total solved.
Week 5: Basic system design concepts (no need to go deep). 5–6 STAR behavioral stories. "Tell me about yourself" practiced.
Week 6: Mock interviews (2–3 full sessions). Company research. Rest before the interview.

Focus distribution: 60% coding, 15% system design basics, 25% behavioral + company prep
📘 Mid-Level (2–5 yrs) — 3 to 4 Week Sprint +
Week 1: Refresh LeetCode patterns. Do 15–20 medium problems across the key patterns. Code in your interview language daily.
Week 2: System design (2–3 full practice designs). Start behavioral stories (7–8 ready).
Week 3: Role-specific depth (use the framework prep apps). Mock interviews x2. Company research.
Week 4 (if available): Weak areas only. Full mock loop (coding + system design + behavioral). Rest.

Focus distribution: 40% coding, 35% system design, 25% behavioral
📙 Senior (5–8 yrs) — 2 to 3 Week Sprint +
Week 1: 10–15 LeetCode mediums (assume strong fundamentals). System design daily — 1 design per day minimum, discuss trade-offs out loud.
Week 2: Leadership behavioral stories (conflict, failure, cross-team, mentoring, ownership). Company LP mapping. Mock x2.
Week 3 (if available): Polish. Full loop mock. Weak areas. Company-specific questions.

Focus distribution: 25% coding, 50% system design, 25% behavioral/leadership

Senior signal: You should be able to discuss trade-offs, not just solutions. "We chose X over Y because of Z constraint."
📕 Staff / Principal (8+ yrs) — 2 to 4 Week Sprint +
Week 1: Architecture-level system design (distributed systems, multi-region, consistency models). Review your top 3 technical achievements with measurable scope.
Week 2: Leadership and organizational influence stories. How you set technical direction. How you dealt with ambiguity at scale.
Week 3: Company-specific prep (their engineering org, their known architectural challenges). Prepare questions that show strategic thinking.
Week 4 (if needed): Coding warmup (don't let this slip). Mock with someone at the same level.

Focus distribution: 15% coding, 50% architecture/design, 35% leadership/org

Staff signal: You should be talking about impact across teams, setting standards others follow, and making decisions with 2–3 year time horizons.

// PHASE 1 — DAILY HABITS
DAILY ROUTINE
How to structure each day during your prep sprint. The session lengths assume 2–3 hours of available prep time per day on top of your job or other commitments.
The 3-Hour Daily Template (Weekday)
Session 1
45 min
Coding problem — Pick one problem from your current pattern focus. Spend 25 min solving independently (no hints). Spend 20 min reviewing the optimal solution and understanding edge cases. Log: did you get it? What would you do differently?
Session 2
60 min
Core topic study — Rotate daily: Mon/Thu = System Design, Tue/Fri = Role-specific depth (framework/DB/infra), Wed/Sat = Behavioral stories + company research. Use the prep apps in this suite for structured content.
Session 3
30 min
Review + consolidation — Re-read yesterday's notes. Run a flashcard session in NotebookLM. Practice saying 1 STAR story out loud. Update your story bank if you thought of a new angle.
Weekly
Saturday
Full mock session (2 hrs) — 45 min coding problem timed, 45 min system design out loud, 30 min behavioral Q&A with Claude/ChatGPT. Identify the one weakest area and make it next week's priority.
Rotation Schedule (3-Week Example)
WeekCodingSystem DesignBehavioral / Company
Week 12 pointers, sliding window, binary search (3–4 problems/day)Read: load balancing, caching, databases. 1 full design (URL shortener)Write 5 STAR stories. Draft "tell me about yourself"
Week 2DFS/BFS on trees, graphs, recursion (2–3 problems/day)2 full designs (news feed, chat). Discuss trade-offs out loudMap stories to company values. Research company tech blog
Week 3DP patterns + review weak areas (1–2 problems/day)1 design focused on your weak area. Mock design interviewFull mock loop x2. Company research final brief. Rest
Mental Rules to Follow Every Day
✓
Never skip the review. Solving a problem without reviewing the optimal solution teaches you nothing. The review IS the learning.
✓
Always say things out loud. Coding and system design are communication tests, not just problem-solving. Narrate your thinking from day one.
✓
One mock per week, minimum. Timed pressure reveals gaps that relaxed study never will. Mock early, mock often.
✓
Track your weak areas. Keep a note with patterns/topics you got wrong. Revisit them twice before the interview.
✓
Stop the day before. No new topics the day before your interview. Review only your notes and stories. Rest. Sleep 8 hours.

AI TOOLS GUIDE
The right AI tool for the right job. Used correctly, these tools can compress 3 weeks of prep into 1. Used wrong (copy-pasting answers without thinking), they create a false sense of readiness.
The AI Toolkit
🤖
Claude / ChatGPT
FREE TIER
General-purpose AI for simulating mock interviews, getting solution explanations, behavioral story feedback, and open-ended Q&A on any topic.
Best for: Mock coding rounds, system design discussion, STAR story critique, "explain this concept differently"
📓
NotebookLM
FREE
Google's AI that answers from YOUR uploaded sources only. Zero hallucination risk. Upload your notes and it becomes a personal tutor grounded in exactly what you've studied.
Best for: Quiz me on my notes, generate flashcards, explain concepts from my study material, audio overview for commute
👥
Pramp
FREE
Free peer mock interview platform. You interview someone, they interview you. Real back-and-forth, real timing pressure. Nothing replaces a human interviewer.
Best for: Final week practice. Coding interviews with a real human. Getting feedback on your communication style
💻
Cursor / Copilot
PAID
AI-enhanced coding environment. Use it to understand patterns and implementations while studying, not during the actual interview.
Best for: Implementing system design components to understand them, debugging your practice solutions, "explain this code"
🧩
NeetCode.io
FREE
The best structured LeetCode resource. Video explanations of every Blind 75 problem + roadmap by topic. Better than LeetCode Discuss for understanding patterns.
Best for: Understanding WHY a solution works. Pattern-focused roadmap. Post-solve review
🎙️
Claude Voice / ChatGPT Voice
FREE
Use the mobile app voice mode to practice speaking your STAR stories and system design explanations. Crucial — most candidates never practice speaking out loud.
Best for: Verbal practice of STAR stories, explaining system design while walking/commuting, "tell me about yourself" practice
Tool Combination Strategy by Week
WeekPrimary AI ToolHow to Use It
Weeks 1–2 (Learning phase)Claude / ChatGPTAsk it to explain concepts you don't understand. "Explain consistent hashing like I'm a mid-level engineer." After solving a problem: "Here's my solution — what's the optimal approach and why is mine suboptimal?"
Weeks 2–3 (Practice phase)NotebookLMUpload your study notes and ask it to quiz you. "Generate 10 system design interview questions from my notes." "Quiz me on my STAR stories." Run the audio overview while commuting.
Week before interviewClaude / ChatGPT + PrampFull mock interviews with Claude (see mock prompts page). At least one real mock on Pramp for live pressure. Critique your behavioral stories.
Night beforeNotebookLMOne review session — ask it to quiz you on your 5 weakest topics. 15 minutes max. Then stop and rest.
The danger of AI-assisted prep: AI gives you answers. Real interviews ask you to produce answers. Always close the AI, wait 10 minutes, then try to reproduce what you learned from scratch. If you can't do it, you didn't learn it.
The right mindset: AI is your tutor, not your proxy. Use it to understand concepts faster, get more practice reps, and stress-test your understanding — not to pre-generate answers you'll try to recall verbatim.

MOCK INTERVIEW PROMPTS
Copy these directly into Claude or ChatGPT. Each prompt is designed to simulate a specific interview type with realistic pressure. Replace the bracketed fields with your info.
Prompt 1 — Full Coding Interview Simulation
CODING MOCK — PASTE INTO CLAUDE / CHATGPT
You are a senior software engineer at [Company Name] conducting a technical coding interview. I am interviewing for a [Senior/Mid/Junior] Software Engineer position. Your behavior: - Ask me exactly ONE coding problem appropriate for this level and company - Do NOT give me any hints unless I am completely stuck and ask for them - Respond only with brief interviewer-style responses (not long explanations) - When I say "I'm done", evaluate my solution: correctness, time/space complexity, edge cases I missed, and what a stronger solution would look like - After evaluation, ask me one follow-up question like a real interviewer would My constraints: - I will write my answer in [Python / JavaScript / Java / TypeScript] - I am targeting [Easy / Medium / Hard] difficulty - Focus area: [arrays + hashing / trees + graphs / dynamic programming / any] Start by introducing yourself briefly as the interviewer, then give me the problem. Do NOT explain it — give it to me the way a real interviewer would.
Prompt 2 — System Design Interview Simulation
SYSTEM DESIGN MOCK — PASTE INTO CLAUDE / CHATGPT
You are a staff engineer at [Company Name] conducting a system design interview. I am a [Senior / Staff] level candidate. Your behavior: - Give me ONE system design question (choose something appropriate for the company — e.g. if it's a fintech, maybe a payment system; if it's social media, maybe a news feed) - Respond as a real interviewer: be terse, probe my assumptions, ask "why did you choose X over Y?", ask about scale, failure modes, and trade-offs - Do NOT explain the solution — ask questions that push me to think deeper - After 30 minutes (or when I say done), give me: 3 things I did well, 3 things I missed or could go deeper on, and what a senior-level answer would have added My background: [2-3 sentences about your experience and level] Start with the question. Keep your responses short — be a real interviewer, not a teacher.
Prompt 3 — Behavioral Interview Simulation
BEHAVIORAL MOCK — PASTE INTO CLAUDE / CHATGPT
You are an engineering manager at [Company Name] conducting a behavioral interview. I am interviewing for [role and level]. Company values to evaluate against: [e.g. Amazon: Ownership, Customer Obsession, Bias for Action / Google: Googleyness, Leadership, Cognitive ability / Startup: autonomy, bias for action, growth mindset] Your behavior: - Ask me 4 behavioral questions one at a time — wait for my full answer before asking the next - After each answer, ask ONE probing follow-up question ("What was the result?", "What would you do differently?", "What was the hardest part?") - After all 4 answers, give me detailed feedback: What signals I showed well, what signals were missing, what a stronger answer would have included, and whether my stories mapped well to the company values My background: [Your years of experience, current role, 1-2 sentence context] Start with the first behavioral question now.
Prompt 4 — STAR Story Critique
STORY CRITIQUE — PASTE INTO CLAUDE / CHATGPT
I'm preparing for an interview at [Company Name] and I want you to critique my STAR story. The question I'm answering: [e.g. "Tell me about a time you failed and what you learned from it"] My answer: [Paste your full STAR story here] Please evaluate on: 1. Is the Situation clear and concise (not too long)? 2. Is my specific Task/responsibility clear — or does it sound like a team effort? 3. Is the Action detailed enough — do I explain my thinking, not just my actions? 4. Is the Result quantified? Does it have real impact? 5. Does this map to [Company's values, e.g. Amazon Ownership LP]? 6. What would you add or cut? 7. What follow-up questions would a real interviewer ask about this story? Be direct and specific. I want honest critique, not encouragement.
Prompt 5 — "Tell Me About Yourself" Coach
SELF INTRO COACH — PASTE INTO CLAUDE / CHATGPT
I'm preparing my "Tell me about yourself" answer for an interview at [Company Name] for a [role + level] position. My background: - Current role: [Your current title and main responsibility] - Years of experience: [X years] - Key past experiences: [2–3 bullet points about relevant past roles/projects] - Why I'm interested in this role: [Your genuine reason] Here's my current answer (or attempt): [Paste your answer, or write "help me build one from scratch"] Please: 1. Rewrite it using the Present → Past → Future formula (90 seconds when spoken) 2. Make it specific to [Company Name] — not a generic answer 3. Point out anything that sounds generic, vague, or off-putting 4. Suggest what to emphasize given the company's focus and values
Prompt 6 — Concept Explainer
CONCEPT EXPLAINER — PASTE INTO CLAUDE / CHATGPT
I'm preparing for a [Senior / Mid] software engineer interview and I need to understand the following concept deeply enough to explain it in an interview and answer follow-up questions: Concept: [e.g. "How does database indexing work?" / "Explain the CAP theorem" / "What is the event loop in Node.js?"] Please explain it: 1. In plain language first (like talking to a smart non-engineer) 2. Then technically — how it actually works internally 3. With a concrete real-world analogy 4. With 3 interview follow-up questions someone might ask, and the ideal answer to each 5. With the common misconceptions candidates have about it After explaining, ask me to explain it back to you in my own words — then critique my explanation.

// FINAL — INTERVIEW DAY
NIGHT BEFORE CHECKLIST
The night before is about consolidation and logistics — not learning. No new topics. No cramming. Execute this checklist then get 8 hours of sleep.
🔧 Technical Logistics (30 min)
✓
Test camera — ensure lighting is in front of you (not behind), background is clean and professional
✓
Test microphone — ask someone to confirm audio quality, or record yourself for 30 seconds and listen back
✓
Test internet connection — run a speed test (speedtest.net). If unstable, use ethernet or position closer to router
✓
Test the video platform they use (Zoom, Meet, Teams) — make sure you can join without install issues
✓
If coding on their platform (CoderPad, HackerRank) — log in now and run a test snippet so there are no surprises
✓
Close all non-essential apps — especially Slack, email, social media. Turn off phone notifications
✓
Have pen and paper ready for system design sketching or note-taking during the interview
✓
Confirm the exact interview time in YOUR timezone — double-check for daylight savings if cross-timezone
🧠 Mental Preparation (30 min)
✓
Review your "tell me about yourself" — say it out loud once, not in your head. Just once.
✓
Review your 3 strongest STAR stories — just the headlines ("the CI/CD outage story", "the disagreement with PM story"). Don't re-read them fully.
✓
Review your 3 smart questions to ask the interviewer — make sure they still feel genuine and specific
✓
Review the company name, the team, and the role title — sounds obvious but interviewers notice when candidates mix up details
✓
Run a 15-minute NotebookLM session — ask it to quiz you on your 3 weakest topics. Stop after 15 minutes.
✓
Remind yourself: interviewing is a skill. One awkward question won't disqualify you. One silence is fine. Thinking out loud is valued.
☀️ Morning of the Interview
✓
Wake up at least 2 hours before the interview — no one is sharp immediately after waking
✓
Eat a real meal beforehand — blood sugar affects thinking clarity more than you expect
✓
Log into the interview platform 5–10 minutes early — not 30 minutes early (creates anxiety), not on time (creates rush)
✓
Have a glass of water at your desk — speaking for 60 minutes makes your throat dry
✓
If you're anxious: take 3 slow deep breaths before joining. This physiologically reduces the stress response.
🚨 In The Interview — Emergency Reminders
If you get a problem you don't know+
Don't panic. Say: "Let me make sure I understand the problem correctly. Can I clarify a few things?" Repeat the problem back in your own words. Ask about constraints. Ask about edge cases. Buying yourself 3–5 minutes of clarification is normal and expected.

Then: think out loud. Say "My first instinct is X, but let me think about the complexity..." Even if you don't find the optimal solution, demonstrating structured thinking under pressure is evaluated positively.
If you go blank on a behavioral question+
Say this: "That's a great question — let me take a moment to think of the best example." Then pause silently for 5–10 seconds. This is fine and professional. Silence means thinking, not failure.

If you truly can't find a perfect story: "I don't have a perfect example of exactly that, but here's the closest situation I've been in..." Then tell a partial story honestly. Partial + honest >> forced + fabricated.
If your system design goes off-track+
Reset with: "Let me step back and make sure I'm addressing the core requirements." Draw a simple box diagram. Start over from requirements → scale → HLD → component detail.

Interviewers for system design are evaluating your thinking process, not your final diagram. A candidate who recovers cleanly from a wrong direction shows more than one who never goes wrong.
What to say at the END of every round+
When they say "Do you have any questions for me?" — always ask 2–3 questions. Saying "I have no questions" is a red flag. It signals either you didn't prepare or you're not genuinely interested.

Use your pre-prepared company-specific questions. A good one: "What does success look like for someone in this role in the first 6 months?" Another: "What's the hardest technical challenge your team is currently working on?"

End every round with: "Thank you — I really enjoyed this conversation." Then send a thank-you email to the recruiter within 2 hours.
// FINAL THOUGHT You are more prepared than you feel. The anxiety you feel the night before is normal and shared by every candidate — including the ones who get offers. Your job in the interview is not to be perfect. It is to be clear, structured, honest, and curious. Those qualities are what interviewers remember.
