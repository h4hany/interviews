// PLANNING — STEP 01
🗓️ When to Schedule
The single most important decision: don't schedule too early (unprepared) or too late (momentum lost). Here's exactly how to time it.
Recommended Lead Time by Level
JUNIOR (0–2 YRS)
New grad / early career
4–6 weeks
Need time for DSA fundamentals, one full framework pass, and behavioral stories. Don't rush this level — the concepts need repetition to stick.
MID-LEVEL (2–5 YRS)
Software engineer / developer
3–4 weeks
You have fundamentals. Focus sprint on system design, company-specific patterns, and behavioral stories tied to your real work. 3 weeks minimum.
SENIOR (5–8 YRS)
Senior / tech lead
2–3 weeks
Deep system design, leadership STAR stories, architecture tradeoffs. You know the tech — the gap is usually communication and scope breadth.
STAFF / PRINCIPAL (8+ YRS)
Staff / principal / architect
2–4 weeks
Org influence, cross-team design, "bar raiser" behavioral calibration. The hardest part is showing scope — start with a story bank sprint.
ALREADY PREPPING
Actively studying right now
1–2 weeks
Polish and company-specific prep only. Study the target company's engineering blog, recent launches, and their specific interview format.
STACK SWITCH
New language / framework
+2–4 weeks
Add this on top of your normal lead time. Language syntax takes 1–2 weeks, framework patterns take another 2–3. Don't shortcut this.
The Scheduling Decision Framework
// GOLDEN RULE
Schedule your first interview (phone screen or recruiter call) at least 2 weeks out so you have time for a focused sprint. If you need to build fundamentals from scratch, use 4–6 weeks and schedule only after you complete at least one full pass of your study plan. Scheduling too early kills your confidence. Scheduling too late kills your momentum.
When a recruiter reaches out: Don't panic-schedule for next week. It's professional and normal to say:

"I'm very interested. I have a couple of commitments in the next two weeks — could we target [3-4 weeks from now] for the first technical screen? I want to make sure I give this the time it deserves."

Recruiters respect this. It signals you take the process seriously and are organized.
What to Do Immediately After Deciding to Interview
1
Define your target clearly
Write down: company, role level, tech stack expected, interview format (coding only? system design? behavioral?). This shapes everything else. A company's careers page, Glassdoor reviews, and Blind app usually tell you the format.
2
Set your interview date first, then build backwards
Pick your target date. Count backwards and assign: Week N = mock interviews, Week N-1 = system design practice, etc. Having a deadline converts vague "I should study" into a daily commitment.
3
Block daily prep time in your calendar
1.5–2 hours per day minimum. Treat it as a non-negotiable meeting. Early morning before work is the most sustainable for most people — no excuses accumulate by morning.
4
Set up your AI tools on Day 1
Open NotebookLM, upload your first sources. Open Claude/ChatGPT and paste your first mock interview prompt. Don't defer the tooling — it pays back immediately.

🔍 Company Research Guide
What to search, where to look, and what to know about any company before you walk into the interview. This is the research that makes you sound genuinely interested instead of memorized.
What to Research — 7 Domains
1
The product & business model
What do they actually build? Who are their customers? How do they make money? Understand the core product deeply enough that you can talk about technical decisions in the context of the business.
Read their "About" page and investor relations (if public)
Use their product yourself if possible — sign up for a free tier
Search: "[Company] business model", "[Company] revenue 2024"
2
Their tech stack & engineering culture
What languages, frameworks, infrastructure do they use? What problems are they solving at scale? This tells you what to emphasize in your answers.
Read their engineering blog (medium.com/@[company], engineering.[company].com)
Check their GitHub profile for open source repos
Search: "[Company] engineering blog", "[Company] tech stack", "[Company] architecture"
Check StackShare, Wappalyzer for tech detection
3
Their interview format & process
What does the actual interview look like? How many rounds? What types of questions? This information is gold — you can prepare exactly what they'll test.
Glassdoor: "[Company] interview" → read last 6-12 months reviews
Blind app: Search the company name + "interview"
Reddit: r/cscareerquestions — search "[Company] interview 2024"
LeetCode Discuss: "[Company] interview questions"
Ask the recruiter directly: "What does the interview loop look like?"
4
Their values & culture
Every company has stated values. Amazon has 16 Leadership Principles. Google has "Googleyness." Learn the specific language they use and map your STAR stories to their vocabulary. Interviewers notice when you use their framework.
Find their values/principles on the careers page
Read their "Life at [Company]" blog if it exists
Prepare 1-2 STAR stories that align to each major value
5
Recent news & launches
What has the company shipped or announced in the last 3-6 months? Referencing a recent launch in "why this company" shows you actually follow them — not just that you need a job.
Google: "[Company] news site:techcrunch.com OR site:theverge.com" (last 6 months)
Their official blog / changelog / product updates page
Their Twitter/X, LinkedIn company page
ProductHunt for recent product launches
6
The team & your interviewers
If you know who's interviewing you, research them briefly. Look at their LinkedIn, GitHub, or any talks/blog posts. You don't need to stalk — you need to find one thing to connect on or one technical topic they care about.
LinkedIn: look at their title, tenure, recent posts
GitHub: any repos, contributions, or talks
YouTube/conference talks: search their name + the company
7
Compensation benchmarks
Know the market rate before the interview so you're not caught off-guard. This matters even if the recruiter handles compensation separately — knowing the range makes you confident.
levels.fyi — the best source for compensation data by level
Glassdoor salary section for the specific role
LinkedIn Salary Insights
Blind: "[Company] TC" (total compensation) discussions

// PLANNING — STEP 03
📚 Study Plan by Phase
A structured, phase-by-phase approach to covering everything — in the right order, at the right depth. Don't study randomly. Follow the phases.
The 5-Phase Study System
PHASE 01
Foundations
Week 1
Language + Core Data Structures
Get fluent in your target language (syntax, OOP, async, error handling). Master: arrays, hash maps, linked lists, stacks, queues, trees, graphs. Know Big O cold. Skip if already comfortable — go straight to Phase 2.

Daily: 1 easy LeetCode + 1 data structure reading. Goal: speak the language fluently.
PHASE 02
Coding Patterns
Weeks 1–2
Algorithm Patterns — Not Just Problems
Master patterns, not individual problems. Two pointers, sliding window, binary search, DFS/BFS, dynamic programming, intervals, monotonic stack. After seeing 3-4 problems per pattern you stop needing to memorize — you recognize the template.

Target: Blind 75 or NeetCode 150. 2 problems/day. ALWAYS explain your approach before coding.
PHASE 03
System Design
Weeks 2–3
High-Level Architecture + Trade-offs
Do 4-5 end-to-end designs: URL shortener, news feed, chat system, rate limiter, ride-sharing. Each design: requirements → capacity estimates → high-level diagram → key components → bottlenecks → trade-offs. Practice speaking while designing — interviewers want to see your thinking, not a finished diagram.

Resources: ByteByteGo, system-design-primer on GitHub. Do 1 design per day, out loud.
PHASE 04
Role Depth
Weeks 2–4
Stack-Specific Deep Dive
Backend: API design, DB indexing, caching, messaging queues. Frontend: framework-specific (React hooks, Angular DI, performance). Full-stack: both + deployment. ML/AI: model serving, embeddings, RAG, evaluation. Use the prep apps in this library for each topic.

Run through the relevant prep app in this collection. Do the quiz questions out loud. Mark topics done.
PHASE 05
Behavioral
Ongoing
STAR Stories + Company Fit
Build 8 versatile stories (achievement, failure, conflict, leadership, ambiguity, cross-team, fast learning, above-and-beyond). Map to company values. Practice "Tell me about yourself" until it takes exactly 90 seconds. Do this in parallel with Phases 2-4 — don't leave it to the last day.

Use the Behavioral prep app. Record yourself on audio at least once — it reveals filler words and pace issues.

// AI TOOLS — OVERVIEW
🧠 AI Tools Overview
The right tool for the right job. Using AI well for interview prep is a superpower. Using it poorly (relying on memorized AI answers instead of understanding) is a trap.
The Tool Map
📓
NotebookLM
STUDY + REVIEW
Google's AI that only answers from sources you upload. Upload your prep notes, get quizzed, generate audio overviews, build flashcards. Your "personal tutor grounded in your own materials."
When: reviewing your own notes, getting quizzed, generating study guides from uploaded content.
🤖
Claude / ChatGPT
MOCK INTERVIEWS + FEEDBACK
Best for: playing the role of a specific company's interviewer, generating behavioral questions, giving STAR story feedback, explaining concepts in different ways, discussing system design trade-offs.
When: mock interviews, story feedback, concept clarification, practice Q&A, generating company-specific questions.
💻
Cursor / Copilot
CODING PRACTICE
Use for: implementing system design components (write a rate limiter, implement LRU cache), understanding code from other solutions, "explain this snippet" in your IDE. Do NOT use to solve LeetCode for you.
When: implementing practice systems, understanding other people's solutions after you've tried yourself.
🎙️
Pramp / Interviewing.io
LIVE PEER MOCKS
Free peer-to-peer mock interviews with real people. Pramp pairs you with another candidate — you interview each other. Best for: timing practice, communication feedback, nerves reduction. Do at least 2 before your real interview.
When: 1–2 weeks before the real interview. Simulate the pressure of a real person watching you think.
📹
Gemini + YouTube
CONCEPT LEARNING
Use Gemini to summarize long YouTube talks (system design explainers, company tech talks). Ask "summarize this YouTube video" and paste the URL. Great for absorbing ByteByteGo content or conference talks efficiently.
When: learning a new system design concept, watching company tech talks efficiently.
🔊
ElevenLabs / Text-to-Speech
PASSIVE REVIEW
Convert your STAR stories or concept summaries to audio. Listen during commute, gym, or walks. Passive review while your hands are busy. Hearing yourself say your stories builds recall confidence.
When: commuting, exercising, doing chores. Passive repetition builds answer fluency.
The Golden Rules of AI-Assisted Prep
⚠️ What NOT to do:

Do NOT ask AI to solve your LeetCode problems for you. You'll memorize solutions without understanding patterns — and blank in the real interview.

Do NOT use AI-generated answers verbatim for behavioral questions. Interviewers hear generic AI-sounding answers constantly. They're trained to probe deeper — and your story will collapse.

Do NOT rely on AI feedback as a substitute for human mocks. AI doesn't penalize bad communication habits, over-explaining, or nervous filler words the way a real person does.
✅ What TO do:

Use AI to understand not memorize. After you solve a problem yourself, use AI to explain edge cases, compare approaches, and challenge your reasoning.

Use AI to generate questions you haven't seen. "Give me 5 more behavioral questions in the style of [Company]" → you answer them, then ask AI for feedback.

Use AI as a sparring partner for system design. Describe your architecture, then ask "What are the weaknesses in this design? What would fail at 10x scale?"

// AI TOOLS — NOTEBOOKLM
📓 NotebookLM Deep Dive
Google's AI study assistant that only answers from your uploaded sources. No hallucinations from the open web — just your materials, explained and quizzed back to you.
Step-by-Step Setup
1
Access & Sign In
Go to notebooklm.google.com — free with any Google account. No subscription needed. Works best in Chrome or Edge.
2
Create Notebooks (One Per Topic)
Click "New notebook." Create one per subject area — don't put everything in one. Suggested notebooks:

• "System Design + Architecture" — upload system design notes, ByteByteGo summaries
• "Behavioral + STAR Stories" — your stories, company values
• "Backend / [Stack]" — framework Q&A, language notes
• "[Company Name] Prep" — job description, Glassdoor notes, company engineering blog
3
Add Sources (What to Upload)
Click "Add source." Accepted formats:

PDFs: Export any Markdown notes to PDF. Upload prep app content as PDF. Upload the job description as PDF.
Google Docs: Copy-paste key content from prep apps into a Google Doc and add the Doc link directly.
YouTube URLs: Paste a system design video URL — NotebookLM will analyze the transcript.
Web pages: Paste a company engineering blog post URL.

Limit: ~50 sources per notebook, ~500K words per source. Keep one notebook per theme.
4
Best Queries to Ask
After uploading, try these queries in the chat box:
"Quiz me on system design — ask me 5 questions, one at a time, and give feedback on my answer before asking the next."
"What are the 5 most important concepts from these sources that I should understand deeply?"
"Generate a structured study guide from these sources organized by topic."
"Based on my behavioral stories, what STAR stories do I have for: conflict, failure, leadership, ownership?"
"Explain [concept] in simple terms using only information from my uploaded sources."
"What gaps do you notice in my preparation based on these materials? What's missing?"
5
Use Audio Overview
Click "Generate audio overview" — NotebookLM creates a podcast-style conversation about your sources. Listen during commute. This is one of the most underused features. Set it up the night before and listen on the morning of your interview for a fast refresh.
NotebookLM Workflow for Specific Company Prep
// COMPANY-SPECIFIC NOTEBOOK WORKFLOW
Day 1: Create notebook "[Company] Interview Prep". Add sources: job description (PDF), company engineering blog (3-4 recent posts as URLs), Glassdoor interview reviews (copy-paste key points into a Google Doc).

Day 2: Ask "Based on this job description and company engineering blog, what technical topics should I focus on? What does this company seem to value?" — Use the answer to prioritize your study plan.

Day 3+: Add your STAR stories as a Google Doc. Ask "Map my STAR stories to this company's values. Which stories are strongest? Which values am I missing coverage for?"

Night before: Generate audio overview. Ask "Give me the 10 most important things to remember for tomorrow's interview based on everything I've uploaded."

// AI TOOLS — MOCK INTERVIEWS
🎭 Mock Interview Setup
How to run a high-quality mock interview using AI — and how to get feedback that actually improves your performance. Done right, 3 AI mocks equals 1 real interview in preparation value.
The 4-Step Mock Interview Protocol
1
Set the scene completely before starting
Before pasting any prompt, clear your workspace: close other tabs, put your phone face down, open a blank doc. Dress like you would for the real interview if doing video. This mental commitment improves the quality of your answers. Tell the AI: company name, role level, interview type. The more specific, the better the simulation.
2
Don't break character during the mock
Resist the urge to ask the AI "is that right?" mid-answer or say "let me rephrase that." In a real interview you don't get do-overs. If you blank, stay in character — say "Let me think for a moment" and work through it. The discomfort of blanking in a mock is much less costly than blanking in the real thing. Treat every mock like it's the final round.
3
Request structured feedback after each answer
After you finish an answer, ask: "Now step out of interviewer mode. Score my answer 1-10 and give me: (1) what I did well, (2) what was weak, (3) what a 10/10 answer would have included, (4) one specific thing to do differently next time." Write the feedback down — don't just read it and move on.
4
End with a full session debrief
After 4-5 questions, ask: "Give me a full debrief of this mock session. What are my top 3 strengths based on this session? What are my top 3 areas to improve before the real interview? What do I need to practice tomorrow?" This meta-feedback across the whole session reveals patterns a per-question score misses.

🌅 Day-Of Protocol
What to do on the day of the interview — from waking up to going live. Small habits here significantly affect your performance and confidence.
Morning Routine (3-4 Hours Before)
✓
Sleep well the night before — Don't set your alarm for 5am to cram. Go to sleep at your normal time or slightly earlier. Cognitive function drops sharply on under-7 hours sleep.
✓
Eat a proper meal — Not too heavy (brain fog) and not skipping (blood sugar crash). Protein + complex carbs. Your brain runs on glucose.
✓
Light physical activity — Even a 10-minute walk increases alertness and reduces cortisol. Don't sit in bed scrolling until the interview starts.
✓
Review your cheatsheet notes for 20 minutes maximum — Just your STAR story headlines, your "tell me about yourself" opening line, and the company's key values. Don't start new topics.
✓
Listen to the NotebookLM audio overview — If you generated one, this is perfect. Passive refresher while eating breakfast or getting ready.

✓
Test camera, microphone, internet connection — Zoom/Google Meet/Teams should be opened and tested. Not just "it worked last week."
✓
Close all unnecessary browser tabs and applications — Notifications off. Slack off. Phone on silent, face down.
✓
Have water nearby — Your throat will get dry. Having water visible removes one nervous variable.
✓

✓

✓

Mental Preparation — The Mindset Shift
// REFRAME THE EXPERIENCE
Most candidates treat an interview as an evaluation where the interviewer holds all the power and they are being judged. This creates performance anxiety and defensive thinking.

Reframe it: You are having a conversation with a peer about interesting technical problems. They want you to succeed — a "no hire" means they wasted hours of their time too. You are evaluating THEM just as much as they're evaluating you.

When you blank on an answer, say: "Let me think through this out loud — my first instinct is X, but let me verify my reasoning..." This is more impressive than a nervous silence. Interviewers don't expect perfection. They expect clear thinking under pressure.
